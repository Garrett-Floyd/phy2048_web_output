    Section 17.3 Collisions

    One useful application of conservation of momentum is analyzing collisions that take place in isolated systems. Even though the energy of an isolated system is conserved it can still transfer between different forms in the system. The external force on an isolated system is zero and internal forces cancel out, so how can this happen? Even though internal forces cancel out, work done by internal forces don’t necessarily have to. For example, consider the system made up of a person standing on ice holding a bowling ball. Since the system has no velocity it has no kinetic energy. If the person throws the bowling ball they will gain speed in opposite directions and increase the kinetic energy of the system above zero. The forces are in equal and opposite directions but they act on different objects with opposite velocity directions so the work done on each is positive. However, total energy stays the same because the person added the kinetic energy by using up an equal amount of chemical energy stored in their body.
    🔗

    Subsection 17.3.1 1D Elastic Collsions

    If the collision undergone by an isolated system conserves kinetic energy then it is said to be an elastic collision.
    🔗

    Theorem 17.3.1.

    Assume an isolated system of two objects undergo a one dimensional elastic collision and don’t exchange mass with each other. Let \(m_{1}\text{,}\) \(v_{1, i}\text{,}\) and \(v_{1, f}\) be the mass, initial speed, and final speed of object one. Likewise let \(m_{2}\text{,}\) \(v_{2, i}\text{,}\) and \(v_{2, f}\) be the mass, initial speed, and final speed of object two. Then
    \begin{equation*} v_{1, f} = \frac{m_{1} - m_{2}}{m_{1} + m_{2}} v_{1, i} + \frac{2m_{2}}{m_{1}+m_{2}} v_{2, i}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    The kinetic energy conservation equation can be written as
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}m_{1}v_{1, i}^{2} + \frac{1}{2}m_{2}v_{2, i}^{2} \amp = \frac{1}{2}m_{1}v_{1, f}^{2} + \frac{1}{2}m_{2}v_{2, f}^{2}\\ \\ m_{1}v_{1, i}^{2} + m_{2}v_{2, i}^{2} \amp = m_{1}v_{1, f}^{2} + m_{2}v_{2, f}^{2}. \end{align*}
    Now we put all the terms for object 1 on one side and all the terms for object 2 on the other.
    \begin{equation*} m_{1}v_{1, i}^{2} - m_{1}v_{1, f}^{2} = m_{2}v_{2, f}^{2} - m_{2}v_{2, i}^{2}. \end{equation*}
    Factoring both sides gives
    \begin{equation*} m_{1}(v_{1, i} - v_{1,f})(v_{1, i} + v_{1, f}) = m_{2}(v_{2, f} - v_{2, i})(v_{2, i} + v_{2, f}). \end{equation*}
    Doing the same for the conservation of momentum equation gives
    \begin{align*} m_{1}v_{1, i} + m_{2}v_{2, i} \amp = m_{1}v_{1, f} + m_{2}v_{2, f} \\ \\ m_{1}(v_{1, i} - v_{1, f}) \amp = m_{2}(v_{2, f} - v_{2, i}). \end{align*}
    Dividing our final expression from the conservation of kinetic energy by our final expression from the conservation of momentum gives
    \begin{align*} \frac{m_{1}(v_{1, i} - v_{1,f})(v_{1, i} + v_{1, f})}{m_{1}(v_{1, i} - v_{1, f})} \amp = \frac{m_{2}(v_{2, f} - v_{2, i})(v_{2, i} + v_{2, f})}{m_{2}(v_{2, f} - v_{2, i})} \\ \\ v_{1, i} + v_{1, f} \amp = v_{2, i} + v_{2, f}\\ \\ v_{1, f} \amp = v_{2, i} + v_{2, f} - v_{1, i}. \end{align*}
    Now we plugg this back in to the origional conservation of momentum equation
    \begin{align*} m_{1}v_{1, i} + m_2v_{2, i} \amp = m_{1}v_{1, f} + m_2(v_{1, i} + v_{1, f} - v_{2, i}) \\ \\ (m_{1}- m_{2})v_{1, i} + 2m_2v_{2, i} \amp = (m_{1} + m_{2})v_{1, f} \\ \\ v_{1, f} \amp = (\frac{m_{1}- m_{2}}{m_{1} + m_{2}})v_{1, i} + (\frac{2m_2}{m_{1} + m_{2}})v_{2, i}. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 17.3.2.

    Assume an isolated system of two objects undergo a one dimensional elastic collision and don’t exchange mass with each other. Let \(m_{1}\text{,}\) \(v_{1, i}\text{,}\) and \(v_{1, f}\) be the mass, initial speed, and final speed of object one. Likewise let \(m_{2}\text{,}\) \(v_{2, i}\text{,}\) and \(v_{2, f}\) be the mass, initial speed, and final speed of object two. Then
    \begin{equation*} v_{2, f} = \frac{2m_{1}}{m_{1}+m_{2}} v_{1, i} + \frac{m_{2} - m_{1}}{m_{1} + m_{2}} v_{2, i}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We could repeat the process followed in the proof of Theorem 17.3.1 but solve for \(v_{2, f}\) instead of \(v_{1, f}\text{.}\) However, there is an easier way to solve for \(v_{2, f}\text{.}\) In the proof of Theorem 17.3.1 we arbitrary picked one of the objects to be labeled 1 and the other to be label 2. The arbitrary choice of labelling a system 1 or 2 can’t change the physics of the situation. So if we now go and swap all the labels between 1 and 2 the equations should still be valid. This gives the desired result.

    Subsection 17.3.2 Inelastic Collisions

    If the collision undergone by an isolated system does not conserve kinetic energy then it is said to be and inelastic collsion. In this case just use conservation of momentum to solve the problem If the collision undergone by an isolated system converts the maximum amount of kinetic energy to some other form then it is said the be a totally inelastic collision. totally inelastic collsions are a special case of inelastic collsion. Totally nelastic collsions occur when two objects stick together after colliding. This scenario gives rise to the following conservation of momentum equation:
    \begin{equation*} m_{1}\vec{v}_{1} + m_{2}\vec{v}_{2} = \left( m_{1} + m_{2} \right)\vec{v}_{3}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 17.3.3 Explosions

    Explosions occur when an object splits into two or more pieces. An example would be a firework rocket. Initially, the firework is one piece, but after the explosion, the firework splits into separate objects, with separate velocities. For explosions, use conservation of momentum in the form
    \begin{equation*} \sum_j m_{j}\vec{v}_{j, i} = \sum_k m_{k}\vec{v}_{k, f} \end{equation*}
    where the indecies j and k run over all the objects that exists before and after the collsion respectively.
