    Section 17.6 Variable Mass Force

    Previously we have use Newton’s Second Law in two forms, given by
    \begin{equation*} \vec{F}_{net} = m \vec{a} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \vec{F}_{net} = \frac{d \vec{p}}{dt}. \end{equation*}
    We used these forms under the assumption that the mass of the system is constant. However, when the mass is not constant these expressions appear to disagree. Which if either is right? The answer to this question is nuanced. There are different answers to this question depending on how you want to interpret things. We will provide one answer which is widely regarded as the most conveinent to work with in practice.
    🔗

    Theorem 17.6.1.

    Let \(v_{rel}\) denote the relative velocity of the mass being transfered between the system and the environment. The equation governing the evolution of systems with variable mass is
    \begin{equation*} \vec{F}_{net} + \vec{v}_{rel} \frac{dm}{dt} = m \vec{a} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    To derive the dynamics of a system with variable mass at an arbitrary time \(t\text{,}\) we will investigate how the system evolves over the infinitesimal time interval \(t\) to \(t + dt\text{.}\) To do this we will embedded the system into the constant mass system made up of the original system and the entering or leaving mass. At an arbitrary time \(t\) the original system has mass \(m\text{.}\) Over an infinitesimal time \(dt\) the original system’s mass will change by an infinitesmal amount \(dm\text{,}\) where \(dm\) is positive if mass is entering the original system and negative if mass is leaving the original system. Over the infinitesimal time \(dt\) the original system’s velocity will also change by an amount \(d \vec{v}\text{.}\) We denote the velocity of the entering or leaving mass as \(\vec{u}\text{.}\) Then the relative velocity of the entering or leaving mass compared to the original system is \(v_{rel} = \left( \vec{u} - \vec{v} \right)\text{.}\) We will break the analysis into two cases.
    🔗
    Case 1 is the case where mass is entering the original system. The initial momentum of the constant mass system is
    \begin{equation*} \vec{p}_{i} = m \vec{v} + \vec{u} dm. \end{equation*}
    The final momentum of the constant mass system is
    \begin{align*} \vec{p}_{f} \amp= \left( m + dm \right) \left( \vec{v} + d \vec{v} \right) \\ \\ \amp = m \vec{v} + m d \vec{v} + \vec{v} dm + dm d \vec{v}. \end{align*}
    The change in momentum \(d \vec{p}\) over the interval \(dt\) is then
    \begin{align*} d \vec{p} \amp = m \vec{v} + m d \vec{v} + \vec{v} dm + dm d \vec{v} - \left( m \vec{v} + \vec{u} dm \right) \\ \\ \amp = m d \vec{v} - \left( \vec{u} - \vec{v} \right)dm + dm d \vec{v} \\ \\ \amp = m d \vec{v} - \vec{v}_{rel} dm + dm d \vec{v} \\ \\ \amp = m d \vec{v} - \vec{v}_{rel} dm \end{align*}
    where we drop the \(dm d \vec{v}\) term in the last line because the product of two infinitesimal values is insignificant in comparison to the terms containing a single infinitesimal. Either form of Newton’s Law applies to the constant mass system so we can write
    \begin{align*} \vec{F}_{net} \amp = \frac{d \vec{p}}{dt} \\ \\ \vec{F}_{net} \amp = m \frac{d \vec{v}}{dt} - \vec{v}_{rel} \frac{dm}{dt} \\ \\ \vec{F}_{net} + \vec{v}_{rel} \frac{dm}{dt} \amp = m \frac{d \vec{v}}{dt} \\ \\ \vec{F}_{net} + \vec{v}_{rel} \frac{dm}{dt} \amp = m \vec{a} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Case 2 is the case where mass is leaving the original system. The inital momentum of the constant mass system is
    \begin{equation*} \vec{p}_{i} = m \vec{v}. \end{equation*}
    The final momentum of the constant mass system is
    \begin{align*} \vec{p}_{f} \amp = \left(m + dm \right) \left(\vec{v} + d \vec{v} \right) - \vec{u}dm \\ \\ \amp = m \vec{v} + m d \vec{v} + \vec{v} dm + dm d\vec{v} - \vec{u} dm. \end{align*}
    Recall that \(dm\) is negative in this case so \(-dm\) is positive. The change in momentum \(d \vec{p}\) over the interval \(dt\) is then
    \begin{align*} dp \amp = m \vec{v} + m d \vec{v} + \vec{v} dm + dm d\vec{v} - \vec{u} dm - m \vec{v} \\ \\ dp \amp = m d \vec{v} + \vec{v} dm + dm d\vec{v} - \vec{u} dm \\ \\ dp \amp = m d \vec{v} - \left( \vec{u} - \vec{v} \right) dm + dm d\vec{v} \\ \\ dp \amp = m d \vec{v} - \vec{v}_{rel} dm + dm d\vec{v} \\ \\ dp \amp = m d \vec{v} - \vec{v}_{rel} dm \end{align*}
    where we drop the \(dm d \vec{v}\) term in the last line because the product of two infinitesimal values is insignificant in comparison to the terms containing a single infinitesimal. Either form of Newton’s Law applies to the constant mass system so we can write
    \begin{align*} \vec{F}_{net} \amp = \frac{d \vec{p}}{dt} \\ \\ \vec{F}_{net} \amp = m \frac{d \vec{v}}{dt} - \vec{v}_{rel} \frac{dm}{dt} \\ \\ \vec{F}_{net} + \vec{v}_{rel} \frac{dm}{dt} \amp = m \frac{d \vec{v}}{dt} \\ \\ \vec{F}_{net} + \vec{v}_{rel} \frac{dm}{dt} \amp = m \vec{a} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Principle 17.6.2. Newton’s Second Law for Variable Mass Systems.

    Let \(v_{rel}\) denote the relative velocity of the mass being transfered between the system and the environment. If we take the interpretation that mass being transfered between the system and the environment causes an external force
    \begin{equation*} \vec{F}_{mass} = \vec{v}_{rel} \frac{dm}{dt} \end{equation*}
    on the system, then Newton’s Second Law in the form
    \begin{equation*} \vec{F}_{net} = m \vec{a} \end{equation*}
    applies to variable mass systems. \(\vec{F}_{mass}\) must be included in \(\vec{F}_{net}\) for this to be true. If multiple simultaneous mass transfers are occuring between the system and the environment, then each mass transfer will exert its own variable mass force on the system.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 17.6.3. Rocket Equation.

    Let the only force acting on a object be the force caused by transfering mass between the system and the environment. Furthermore, suppose that the relative velocity of the transfered mass is constant with respect to the system. Let \(\vec{v_{i}}\) and \(\vec{v_{f}}\) denote the inital and final velocities of the object. Let \(m_{i}\) and \(m_{f}\) denote the initial and final mass of the system. Then
    \begin{equation*} \vec{v_{f}} = \vec{v_{i}} - \vec{v}_{rel} \ln \left( \frac{m_{i}}{m_{f}} \right). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Because the variable mass force is the only force acting on the system, Principle 17.6.2 gives
    \begin{align*} \vec{v}_{rel} \frac{dm}{dt} \amp = m \frac{d \vec{v}}{dt} \\ \\ \vec{v}_{rel} \frac{1}{m} \frac{dm}{dt} \amp = \frac{d \vec{v}}{dt} \\ \\ \vec{v}_{rel} \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} \frac{1}{m} \frac{dm}{dt} dt \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} \frac{d \vec{v}}{dt} dt \\ \\ \vec{v}_{rel} \int_{m_{i}}^{m_{f}} \frac{dm}{m} \amp = \int_{\vec{v}_{i}}^{\vec{v}_{f}} d \vec{v} \\ \\ \vec{v}_{rel} \Big[ \ln (m) \Big] \Bigg|_{m_{i}}^{m_{f}} \amp = \vec{v}_{f} - \vec{v}_{i} \\ \\ \vec{v}_{rel} \ln \left( \frac{m_{f}}{{m_{i}}} \right) \amp = \vec{v}_{f} - \vec{v}_{i} \\ \\ - \vec{v}_{rel} \ln \left( \frac{m_{i}}{{m_{f}}} \right) \amp = \vec{v}_{f} - \vec{v}_{i} \\ \\ \vec{v_{f}} \amp = \vec{v_{i}} - \vec{v}_{rel} \ln \left( \frac{m_{i}}{m_{f}} \right) \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Exercises Exercises

    1. Derivation of Velocity from Rocket Propulsion.

    Suppose an isolated rocket in outer space (negligible gravity) has given initial velocity \(+v_{i}\) and given initial mass \(M_{i}\text{.}\) It then converts some of its fuel into exhaust gas and pushes it out the back, so the rocket’s mass without the exhaust gas is only \(M_{f}\text{.}\) An observer onboard the rocket sees the exhaust leaving with given steady velocity \(-v_{exh}\) relative to the rocket, but ground control sees the true velocity of the exhaust relative to the ground, which is slower because the exhaust was being carried along on the platform of the rocket, and we already know the platform’s velocity was \(\fillinmath{X}\text{.}\) Therefore, true ground velocity of exhaust
    \begin{equation*} = -v_{exh}+v_{platform} = -v_{exh}+ \fillinmath{X}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since we have here an explosion question, we can use the Law of Conservation of Momentum to solve for the change in the rocket’s velocity, \(v_{f}-v_{i}\text{,}\) as it pushes a small packet of exhaust out the back. Then, we will integrate that equation to find the rocket’s velocity after it has pushed millions of small packets of exhaust out the back. We’ll assume the rocket is travelling in the x-direction only, so all the y-components are zero.
    🔗
    1. For collision/explosion problems, we use the Law of Conservation of Momentum because the work done by external/non-conservative forces is too complicated to calculate. Let’s write the Law of Conservation of Momentum:
      \begin{equation*} {\vec{p}_{total}}_{i} = {\vec{p}_{total}}_{f} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Now sort out the givens and unknowns. For a Conservation of Momentum problem, we need to check on givens for the masses, the initial velocity components for all objects, and the final velocity components for all objects. A diagram sometimes helps for putting the plus or minus signs on to indicate directions: Rocket alone (without exhaust gas) Exhaust gas mass alone
      \begin{equation*} m_{1} = M_{\fillinmath{X}\ } \end{equation*}
      (mass of rocket not including mass of exhaust)
      \begin{equation*} m_{2} = \fillinmath{X} - M_{f} \end{equation*}
      (mass of exhaust in terms of \(M_{i}\) and \(M_{f}\))
      \begin{equation*} v_{1i}= < \fillinmath{X}, 0 > \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} v_{2i}= < \fillinmath{X}, 0 > \end{equation*}
      (since exhaust mass was initially part of the rocket, it initially shared the rocket’s velocity)
      \begin{equation*} v_{1f}= < v_{f}, 0 > \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} v_{2f}= < -v_{exh}+\fillinmath{X}, 0\ > \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Expand the Law of Conservation of Momentum so we can see where to plug in the givens. Since there are \(\ \fillinmath{X}\ \) objects, we will need to break the \(p_{total}\) into \(\ \fillinmath{X}\ \) sources of momentum:
      \begin{equation*} {\vec{\ p_{1}}}_{i} + \ \fillinmath{X}\ = \ \fillinmath{X}\ + {\vec{\ p_{2}}}_{f} \end{equation*}
      then do the x and y components of the equation:
      \begin{equation*} {p_{1}}_{ix} + \ \fillinmath{X}\ = \ \fillinmath{X}\ + {p_{2}}_{fx} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} M_{f}\ \fillinmath{X}\ + M_{exhaust}v_{i} = M_{f}\ \fillinmath{X}\ + M_{exhaust}\left( - v_{exh} + v_{i} \right) \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} M_{f}\ \fillinmath{X}\ + \left( \ \fillinmath{X}\ - M_{f} \right)v_{i} = M_{f}\ \fillinmath{X}\ + \left( \ \fillinmath{X}\ - M_{f} \right)\left( - v_{exh} + v_{i} \right) \end{equation*}
      We are going to set \(\left( M_{f} - M_{i} \right) = \mathrm{\Delta}M\) , just to make it easier to use multiple \(\mathrm{\Delta}M\) ’s in a while:
      \begin{equation*} M_{f}v_{i} + \left( - \mathrm{\Delta}\ \fillinmath{X}\ \right)\ v_{i} = M_{f}v_{f} + \left( - \mathrm{\Delta}\ \fillinmath{X}\ \right)\ \left( - v_{exh} + v_{i} \right) \end{equation*}
      Now distribute terms through the bracket to get:
      \begin{equation*} M_{f}v_{i} - \mathrm{\Delta}M\ v_{i} = M_{f}v_{f} + \mathrm{\Delta}M\ \fillinmath{X}\ - \mathrm{\Delta}M\ \fillinmath{X}\ \end{equation*}
      Cancelling the common term from both sides gives:
      \begin{equation*} M_{f}v_{i} = M_{f}v_{f} + \mathrm{\Delta}Mv_{exh} \end{equation*}
      Finally, solving for the change in the velocity due to pushing one small packet of exhaust out the back:
      \begin{equation*} M_{f}v_{f} - M_{f}\ \fillinmath{X}\ = - \mathrm{\Delta}Mv_{exh} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} M_{f}\left( v_{f} - \ \fillinmath{X}\ \right) = - \mathrm{\Delta}Mv_{exh} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \mathrm{\Delta}v = - \frac{1}{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }\mathrm{\Delta}M\ \fillinmath{X}\ \end{equation*}
      y-components are all zero since it is an x-direction problem only Next, let’s take the limit of the change in velocity when the mass of the exhaust is very small, \(\mathrm{\Delta}M \rightarrow 0\text{.}\) This turns all the \(\Delta\)’s into derivative symbol \(d\)’s:
      \begin{equation*} \lim_{\mathrm{\Delta}M \rightarrow 0}{\mathrm{\Delta}v} = \lim_{\mathrm{\Delta}M \rightarrow 0}{- \frac{1}{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }\mathrm{\Delta}M\ \fillinmath{X}\ } \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} dv = - \frac{1}{M}dMv_{exh} \end{equation*}
      Now, to find the final velocity after millions of packets of exhaust have been expelled, we can take the integral of both sides to solve for the final v:
      \begin{equation*} \int_{}^{}{dv} = \int_{}^{}{- \frac{1}{M}dMv_{exh}} \end{equation*}
      Let’s assume the rocket began with mass \(M_{1}\) and velocity \(v_{1}\text{,}\) and ended with mass \(M_{2}\) and velocity \(v_{2}\text{.}\) Since the variable of integration on the left side is \(\fillinmath{X}\text{,}\) and the variable of integration on the right side is \(\fillinmath{X}\text{,}\) we can use these assumed values for the limits of integration:
      \begin{equation*} \int_{\fillinmath{X}\ }^{\fillinmath{X}\ }{dv} = \int_{\fillinmath{X}\ }^{\fillinmath{X}\ }{- \frac{1}{M}dMv_{exh}} \end{equation*}
      Factor out the constant:
      \begin{equation*} \int_{\fillinmath{X}\ }^{\fillinmath{X}\ }{dv} = - v_{exh}\int_{\fillinmath{X}\ }^{\fillinmath{X}\ }{\frac{1}{M}dM} \end{equation*}
      Doing the definite integrals, we get:
      \begin{equation*} v\left| \begin{matrix} \fillinmath{X}\ \\ \ \\ \fillinmath{X}\ \end{matrix} \right.\ = - v_{exh}\fillinmath{X}\ \left| \begin{matrix} \fillinmath{X}\ \\ \ \\ \fillinmath{X}\ \end{matrix} \right.\ \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} v_{2} - v_{1} = - v_{exh}(\ln{\fillinmath{X}\ } - \ln{\fillinmath{X}\ }) \end{equation*}
      Distributing the minus sign inside the brackets:
      \begin{equation*} v_{2} - v_{1} = + v_{exh}( - \ln{\fillinmath{X}\ } + \ln{\fillinmath{X}\ }) \end{equation*}
      Using the rules of logarithms, we can combine the ln’s:
      \begin{equation*} v_{2} - v_{1} = v_{exh}(\ln\frac{\fillinmath{X}\ }{\fillinmath{X}\ }) \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} v_{2} = \fillinmath{X} + v_{exh}(\ln\frac{\fillinmath{X}\ }{M_{2}\ }) \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    From the equation for rocket propulsion, we can see:
    🔗
    1. 1. If \(v_{exh}\) is large (faster exhaust), that tends to \(\fillinmath{X}\) the final \(v_2\) of the rocket.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. 2. If \(M_{1}\) is large (more initial fuel available), that tends to \(\fillinmath{X}\) the final \(v_2\) of the rocket.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. 3. If \(M_{2}\) is small (less fuel remaining at end), that tends to \(\fillinmath{X}\ \) the final \(v_2\) of the rocket.
      🔗
