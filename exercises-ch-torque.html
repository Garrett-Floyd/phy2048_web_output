    Torque and angular acceleration

    1. Derivation of Relationship Between Torque and Angular Acceleration.

    Torque is known as the capacity to rotate an object. In this exercise, we will see how a specific definition for torque is useful for determining how quickly an object’s rotation increases.
    1. Because pushing an object perpendicular to the direction it is hinged generates the most rotation for the force, torque is defined using an operation called the cross product. Unlike the dot product, which multiplies one vector by the parallel component of a second vector, the cross product multiplies one vector by the perpendicular component of a second vector. So, instead of the \(\cos (\Delta \theta )\) used in the dot product, the cross product contains \(\fillinmath{X} ( \Delta \theta )\text{:}\)
      🔗
      The magnitude of dot product of \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) is given by multiplying \(\vec{A}\) by the parallel component of \(\vec{B}\text{:}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} |\vec{A}|(|\vec{B}|\cos{(\Delta\theta)}) \end{equation*}
      🔗
      The magnitude of cross product of \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) is given by multiplying \(\vec{A}\) by the perpendicular component of \(\vec{B}\text{:}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} |A|(|B| \fillinmath{X} (\Delta \theta)) \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Since the torque depends on how much force you apply to an object (force = \(\vec{F}\)), and also how far away from the hinge you push the object (displacement from hinge 0 to push location = \(\vec{r}\)), torque (\(\vec{\tau}\)) is defined as the cross product of vector \(\vec{r}\) and vector \(\vec{F}\text{:}\)
      🔗
      \begin{gather*} \vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F} \\ \left| \, \vec{\tau} \, \right| = \left| \, \vec{r} \times \vec{F} \, \right| =r F \sin{\Delta\theta} \end{gather*}
      🔗
      If the force is spinning the object counterclockwise around the hinge, we call this a positive torque (because the rule “lefty-loosey” implies this would cause a screw to travel out of the page). However, if the force is spinning the object \(\fillinmath{XXXX}\) around the hinge, that would be a negative torque (because the rule “righty-tighty” implies this would cause a screw to burrow down into the page).
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \begin{equation*} \left| \vec{\tau} \right| = r(\fillinmath{X} a)\sin{\Delta\theta} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Group the acceleration with the \(\sin{\Delta\theta}\text{:}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \left| \vec{\tau} \right| = r \fillinmath{X} (a\sin{\Delta\theta}) \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. What does the \((a\sin{\Delta\theta})\) mean? We can figure out which acceleration component that corresponds to. We’ll draw the r,t,z axes for circular motion and look at which axis the \((a\sin{\Delta\theta})\) is going along:
      🔗
      So, is \((a\sin{\Delta\theta})\) the radial component, \(a_r\text{,}\) or the tangential component, \(a_t\text{,}\) of the acceleration? \(\fillinmath{XXXX}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Now we can sub out \((a\sin{\Delta\theta})\) for \(a_t\text{:}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \left| \vec{\tau} \right| = rm \fillinmath{X} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    6. But we know from rotational kinematics that tangential acceleration \(a_t\) is related to angular acceleration \(\alpha\) and the radius \(r\) of the circle by:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} a_{t} = r \fillinmath{X} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    7. Subbing in for \(a_t\text{,}\) we now see torque is related to the angular acceleration \(\alpha\text{:}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \left| \vec{\tau} \right| = rm(r \fillinmath{X} ) \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    8. Grouping the r’s together,
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \left| \vec{\tau} \right| = mr^{2} \alpha \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    9. The quantity \(mr^{2}\) is defined as the moment of intertia \(I\) of a point sized mass \(m\text{:}\) So, subbing out \(mr^{2} = I\text{,}\) we get:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \left| \vec{\tau} \right| = \fillinmath{X} \alpha \end{equation*}
      🔗
      This looks very similar to Newton’s Second Law \(\left| \, \vec{F} \, \right| = m \left| \, \vec{a} \, \right|\text{,}\) except this tells you the angular acceleration instead of the rectangular acceleration. \(\vec{\tau}\) plays the role of \(\vec{F}\text{,}\) \(I\) plays the role of \(m\) , and \(\alpha\) plays the role of \(\left| \, \vec{a} \, \right|\text{.}\)
      🔗
      In practice, you will have more than one force acting on your object, so you get a net torque (sum of the positive/counterclockwise and negative/clockwise torques) causing the total counterclockwise/clockwise angular acceleration:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \left| \vec{\tau}_{net} \right| = \left| {\vec{\tau}}_{1} + {\vec{\tau}}_{2} + {\vec{\tau}}_{3} + \ldots \right|= I\alpha \end{equation*}
      🔗
      If the net torque has a \(\fillinmath{X}\) sign, the angular acceleration is counterclockwise. If the net torque has a \(\fillinmath{X}\) sign, the angular acceleration is clockwise.
      🔗
      🔗

    Static Equilibrium

    3. Static equilibrium and the Rigid Body Diagram.

    If an object is in the special case of static equilibrium, that is a new hidden given. It means the object is permanently at rest, and also not rotating. This means it has zero rectangular acceleration \(\vec{a} = 0\) and zero angular acceleration \(\alpha = 0\text{.}\) Therefore, in this special case, the \(\vec{F_{net}}\) is \(\fillinmath{X}\text{,}\) and the \(\vec{\tau_{net}}\) is also \(\fillinmath{X}\text{.}\) Here is a list of the general steps for solving a static equilibrium problem (after that, we will apply the steps in the context of an example):
    🔗
    Step 1. Draw a “rigid-body diagram” of the object. Draw all forces acting on the object. This is just an free body diagram, except that now we must draw the full shape of the object, and draw the forces from the actual locations they pull on the object. However, the force of gravity is still drawn acting on the center dot, because that represents the center of mass.
    🔗
    Step 2. Pick axes and an origin location (which will be the hinge). Draw the displacements from the hinge to the locations the forces act. Draw the angles between the displacements to the forces and the forces themselves, since those angles appear in the torques. Draw x and y axes like in an free body diagram, but in addition, label an origin 0 which will represent the hinge. (Trick: put the origin at a location where there are many forces that aren’t givens, because if there’s a zero displacement from the origin to a force, it contributes \(\vec{0} \times \vec{F} = \vec{0}\) of torque.) Why are we allowed to pick anywhere for the hinge origin? Because we don’t want the object to rotate at all, no matter where it is hinged!
    🔗
    Step 3. Find the symbolic \(F_{net,x}\) and \(F_{net,y}\) the same as usual for an free body diagram problem. Then, sub in \(\vec{F}_{net} = ma = m(0) = 0\) since that is a hidden given in static equilibrium.
    🔗
    Step 4. Find the symbolic \(\vec{\tau}_{net}\) by adding up the torque caused by each force on the diagram (use a + sign for the torque if a force would spin the object counterclockwise, or a - sign for clockwise). Then, sub in \(\vec{\tau}_{net} = I\alpha = I(0) = 0\) since that is a hidden given in static equilibrium.
    🔗
    Step 5. We now have a system of three equations from steps 3 and 4. Use algebra to substitute the equations into each other and solve for the requested unknowns.
    🔗
    Here is an example problem with several different forces:
    🔗
    In Figure 20.2.5 a uniform ladder 12 meters long rests against a vertical frictionless wall. The ladder weighs 400 newtons and makes an angle \(\theta\text{,}\) of 55 degrees with the floor. A man weighing 741 newtons climbs slowly up the ladder. When he has climbed to a point that is 7.8 meters from the base of the ladder, the ladder starts to slip. What is the coefficient of static friction between the floor and the ladder?
    🔗
    Step 1. Draw a “rigid-body diagram” of the object. Draw all forces acting on the object.
    🔗
    Step 2. Pick axes and an origin location (which will be the hinge). Draw the displacements from the hinge to the locations the forces act. Draw the angles between the displacements to the forces and the forces themselves, since those angles appear in the torques. We should put the origin 0 in the lower right corner, because there are \(\fillinmath{X}\) unknown forces there that will provide zero torque, whereas if we put the origin in the upper left corner, there would only be \(\fillinmath{X}\) unknown force that will provide zero torque. Making more zeroes with our choice makes the problem easier!
    🔗
    Step 3. Find the symbolic \(F_{net,x}\) and \(F_{net,y}\) the same as usual for an FBD problem. Then, sub in \(\vec{F}_{net} = ma = m(0) = 0\) since that is a hidden given in static equilibrium.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{N,wall} = \left\langle + F_{N,wall},0 \right\rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{grav,person} = \left\langle 0,{- F}_{grav,person} \right\rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{grav,beam} = \Big\langle \fillinmath{X} \ , \fillinmath{X} \Big\rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{grav,beam} = \Big\langle \fillinmath{X} ,\fillinmath{X} \Big\rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{N,ground} = \Big\langle \fillinmath{X} , \fillinmath{X} \Big\rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{fs} = \Big\langle \fillinmath{X} , \fillinmath{X} \Big\rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Sums of x components:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{net,x} = \fillinmath{X} + 0 + 0 + 0 - F_{fs,ground} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0 = \fillinmath{X} - F_{fs,ground} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F_{fs,ground} = \fillinmath{X} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu_{s,ground}F_{N,ground} = F_{N,wall} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Sums of y components:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{net,y} = 0 - \fillinmath{X} - F_{grav,beam} + F_{N,ground} + 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0 = \fillinmath{X} - F_{grav,beam} + F_{N,ground} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} + F_{N,ground} = + F_{grav,person} + F_{grav,beam} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    At this point, we see we are given \(F_{grav,person}\) and \(F_{grav,beam}\text{,}\) which is not enough to solve for \(\mu_{s,ground}\text{.}\) So, we need to do step 4 to get another piece of info (to help us get \(F_{N,wall}\))
    🔗
    Step 4. Find the symbolic \(\vec{\tau}_{net}\) by adding up the torque caused by each force on the diagram (use a + sign for the torque if a force would spin the object counterclockwise, or a - sign for clockwise). Then, sub in \(\vec{\tau}_{net} = I\alpha = I(0) = 0\) since that is a hidden given in static equilibrium.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{\tau}}_{N,wall} = \fillinmath{X}r_{N,wall}F_{N,wall}\sin\theta\ \text{(since clockwise)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{\tau}}_{grav,person} = + r_{grav,person}F_{grav,person}\sin{(90{^\circ} - \theta)}\ \text{(since counterclockwise)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{\tau}}_{grav,beam} = \fillinmath{X}r_{grav,beam}\fillinmath{X}\sin\left( \fillinmath{X} \right)\ \text{(since counterclockwise)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{\tau}}_{N,ground} = 0\ (since\ r_{N,ground} = 0) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{\tau}}_{fs} = \fillinmath{X}\ (\text{since} \ r_{fs} = 0) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Sum of torques:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{\tau}}_{net} = - r_{N,wall}F_{N,wall}\sin\theta + \fillinmath{X} + r_{grav,beam}F_{grav,beam}\sin(90{^\circ} - \theta) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0 = - r_{N,wall}F_{N,wall}\sin\theta + \fillinmath{X} + r_{grav,beam}F_{grav,beam}\sin(90{^\circ} - \theta) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} r_{N,wall}F_{N,wall}\sin\theta = \fillinmath{X} + r_{grav,beam}F_{grav,beam}\sin(90{^\circ} - \theta) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F_{N,wall} = \frac{1}{\fillinmath{X}\sin\theta}\left\lbrack \fillinmath{X} + r_{grav,beam}F_{grav,beam}\sin(90{^\circ} - \theta) \right\rbrack \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now it’s time to sub in our numbers!
    🔗
    Step 5. We now have a system of three equations from steps 3 and 4. Use algebra to substitute the equations into each other and solve for the requested unknowns.
    🔗
    Lets sub into the torque equation first:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F_{N,wall} = \frac{1}{(12m)\sin{55{^\circ}}}\left\lbrack (7.8m)\left( \fillinmath{X} \right)\sin\left( 90{^\circ} - \fillinmath{X} \right) + \left( \fillinmath{X} \right)(400N)\sin\left( 90{^\circ} - \fillinmath{X} \right) \right\rbrack \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F_{N,wall} = 477.3N \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Let’s sub into the \(F_{net,y}\) next:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {+ F}_{N,ground} = + 741N + \fillinmath{X} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F_{N,ground} = 1141N \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Finally, let’s sub into the \(F_{net,x}\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu_{s,ground}F_{N,ground} = F_{N,wall} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu_{s,ground} = \frac{F_{N,wall}}{F_{N,ground}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu_{s,ground} = \frac{\fillinmath{X}}{\fillinmath{X}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu_{s,ground} = 0.42 \end{equation*}
    🔗
