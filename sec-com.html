    Section 17.5 Center of Mass

    Imagine you want to analyze a system consisting of a large number or particles. It would be quite tedious to keep track of them individually. In some cases the system you want to analyze is an extended body like a chair and analyzing each atom isn’t even feasible. In cases like this it is often good enough to analyze the motion of the systems center of mass (com) instead of every particle in the system.
    🔗

    Principle 17.5.1.

    For a system of particles the com is defined as the vector:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{r}_{com} = \frac{\sum_i m_i \vec{r}_i}{M} \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} M = {\sum_i m_i} \end{equation*}
    and the index \(i\) ranges over all particles in the system.
    🔗
    🔗
    If no mass enters or leaves the system then the system is said to be closed. If the system is closed then \(M\) is constant.
    🔗

    Theorem 17.5.2.

    For a closed system, the velocity of the center of mass is given by
    \begin{equation*} \vec{v}_{com} = \frac{\sum_i m_i \vec{v}_i}{M} \quad . \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Taking the time derivative of Principle 17.5.1 with \(M\) being constant gives the desired result.
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 17.5.3.

    Let \(\vec{P} = \sum_i \vec{p}_i\) be the total linear momentum of the system, and assume that the system is closed. Then
    \begin{equation*} \vec{P} = M\vec{v}_{com} \quad . \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Rearranging Theorem 17.5.2 gives
    \begin{equation*} M \vec{v}_{com} = \sum_i m_i \vec{v}_i = \sum_i \vec{p}_i = \vec{P} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Corollary 17.5.3 says that we can calculate the total linear momentum of a system of particles by pretending that the system is a single partilce of mass \(M\) located at the center of mass.
    🔗

    Theorem 17.5.4.

    For a closed system the acceleration of the center of mass is given by
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a}_{com} = \frac{\sum_i m_i \vec{a}_i}{M} \quad . \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Taking the time derivative of Theorem 17.5.4 with \(M\) being constant gives the desired result.
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 17.5.5.

    For a closed system
    \begin{equation*} \vec{F}_{net} = M \vec{a}_{com} \quad . \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Rearranging Theorem 17.5.4 gives
    \begin{equation*} M \vec{a}_{com} = \sum_i m_i \vec{a}_i = \sum_i \vec{F}_i = \vec{F}_{net} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Corollary 17.5.5 says that we can apply Newton’s second law to a system of particles by pretending that the system is a single particle of mass \(M\) located at the center of mass. We have actually already been secretly been doing this for extended objects throughout the course so far.
    🔗

    Corollary 17.5.6.

    For a closed system
    \begin{equation*} \vec{F}_{net} = \frac{d \vec{P}}{dt} \quad . \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Taking the time deriavtive of Corollary 17.5.3 gives
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d \vec{P}}{dt} = \frac{d (M \vec{v}_{com})}{dt} = M\frac{d \vec{v}_{com}}{dt} = M \vec{a}_{com} \quad . \end{equation*}
    Then by Corollary 17.5.5 we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d \vec{P}}{dt} = M \vec{a}_{com} = \vec{F}_{net} \quad . \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    1. Formula for Center of Mass.

    We’ve seen previously that momentum of an isolated system is conserved. However, if there are too many particles in the system, it would be difficult to add so many sources to get the total momentum. To examine the motion of large groups of particles, we need to create a definition for where the center of mass of the system is. This is based on averaging.
    🔗
    1. Suppose you had a simple isolated system of two grains of wheat. One grain is at x=0.0m and one grain is at x=2.0m. What would you say the average x position (\(\overline{x}\)) of the system was?
      \begin{equation*} \overline{x} = \frac{0m + \ \fillinmath{X}\ }{2} = \ \fillinmath{X}\ \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Suppose you had a simple isolated system of three grains of wheat. One grain is at x=0.0m, one grain is at x=4.0m, and one grain is at x=5.0m. What would you say the average x position (\(\overline{x}\)) of the system was?
      \begin{equation*} \overline{x} = \frac{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }{3} = \ \fillinmath{X}\ \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Suppose you had a simple isolated system of four grains of wheat. One grain is at x=1.0m, and three grains are all at x=5.0m. What would you say the average x position (\(\overline{x}\)) of the system was?
      \begin{equation*} \overline{x} = \frac{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }{\ \fillinmath{X}\ } = \ \fillinmath{X}\ \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Suppose you had an isolated system of 101 grams of flour. 100 grams are making up a cake at x=1.00m, and one gram is at x=5.00m. What would you say the average x position (\(\overline{x}\)) of the system was, to 3 significant figures?
      \begin{equation*} \overline{x} = \frac{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }{\ \fillinmath{X}\ } = \ \fillinmath{X}\ \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Suppose you had an isolated system of flour. 200 grams are making up a cake at x=2.00m, 50 grams are making up a cake at x=3.00m, and 350 grams are making up a cake at x=5.00m. What would you say the average x position (\(\overline{x}\)) of the system was, to 3 significant figures?
      \begin{equation*} \overline{x} = \frac{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }{\ \fillinmath{X}\ } = \ \fillinmath{X}\ \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    So, in general, to get the average x position, we need to take the \(\ \fillinmath{X}\) (in kilogram) of the each object and multiply that by the \(\ \fillinmath{X}\ \) (in m) of that object, add all the answers together, and then divide by the grand total \(\ \fillinmath{X}\ \) of all the objects together. This is called a mass-weighted average, and we also call that the “center of mass” or “centroid”:
    \begin{equation*} \overline{x} = \frac{m_{1}x_{1} + m_{2}x_{2} + \ \fillinmath{X}\ + \ldots + m_{N}x_{N}}{m_{1} + m_{2} + \ \fillinmath{X}\ + \ldots + m_{N}} \end{equation*}
    Writing the sum using the sum symbol,
    \begin{equation*} \overline{x} = \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{N}{m_{i}x_{i}}}{\sum_{i = 1}^{N}m_{i}} \end{equation*}
    If we had been calculating the center of mass along the y coordinate, the equation would be:
    \begin{equation*} \overline{y} = \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{N}{m_{i}y_{i}}}{\sum_{i = 1}^{N}m_{i}} \end{equation*}
    Finally, if we had been calculating the center of mass along the z coordinate, the equation would be:
    \begin{equation*} \overline{z} = \frac{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }{\ \fillinmath{X}\ } \end{equation*}
    So, the position in 3-D of the center of mass is:
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{r}}_{c.o.m.} = \left\langle \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{N}{m_{i}x_{i}}}{\sum_{i = 1}^{N}m_{i}},\frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{N}{m_{i}y_{i}}}{\sum_{i = 1}^{N}m_{i}},\frac{\sum_{i = 1}^{N}{m_{i}z_{i}}}{\sum_{i = 1}^{N}m_{i}} \right\rangle \end{equation*}
    In the limit as the masses of the individual objects shrink towards zero, the mass distribution becomes a smooth, continuous object instead of many discrete objects. In that limit, all the m’s shrink into infinitesimal dm’s, and we use an integral to do the addition of all the tiny masses:
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{r}}_{c.o.m.} = \left\langle \frac{\int_{}^{}{x\ dm}}{\int_{}^{}{dm\ }},\frac{\int_{}^{}{y\ dm}}{\int_{}^{}{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }},\frac{\int_{}^{}{\ z\ \fillinmath{X}}}{\int_{}^{}{dm\ }} \right\rangle \end{equation*}
    In practice, each tiny mass dm is computed by multiplying its tiny volume dV by its density \(\rho\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{r}}_{c.o.m.} = \left\langle \frac{\int_{}^{}{x\ \rho\ dV}}{\int_{}^{}{\rho\ dV\ }},\frac{\int_{}^{}{y\ \rho\ dV}}{\int_{}^{}{\ \fillinmath{X}\ \ dV\ }},\frac{\int_{}^{}{z\ \rho\ \ \fillinmath{X}\ }}{\int_{}^{}{\rho\ dV\ }} \right\rangle \end{equation*}
    If you’re integrating using Cartesian coordinates, the volume of a tiny rectangular volume is dx dy dz.
    \begin{equation*} {\vec{r}}_{c.o.m.} = \left\langle \frac{\int_{}^{}{x\ \rho\ }\ dx\ dy\ dz}{\int_{}^{}{\rho\ dx\ dy\ dz\ }},\frac{\int_{}^{}{y\ \rho\ \ dx\ \ \fillinmath{X}\ \ \ dz}}{\int_{}^{}{\ \fillinmath{X}\ \ dx\ dy\ dz\ }},\frac{\int_{}^{}{z\ \rho \ \ \fillinmath{XXX}\ \ }}{\int_{}^{}{\rho\ dx\ dy\ dz\ }} \right\rangle \end{equation*}
    Doing a triple integral over dx, dy, and dz is something you do in Calculus 3, so you don’t have to do one right now.
