    Section 18.2 Circular Motion

    Now we want to relate linear and angular kinematics for circular motion, but first we need to take a detour to further explore polar coordinates.
    🔗

    Subsection 18.2.1 Relating Cartesian and Polar Coordinate Systems

    Unit vectors point in the direction that increase the coordinate they’re associated with. In the cartesian coordinate system the \(\hat{x}\) unit vector points in the direction of increasing \(x\text{,}\) and the \(\hat{y}\) unit vector points in the direction of increasing \(y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Cartesian coordinates are special because the unit vectors point in the same direction everywhere (They don’t depend on the the position \(\left( x, y \right)\)). Polar coordinates are defined by the radius \(r\) and angle \(\theta\) where \(\theta\) is in radians.
    🔗
    Unit vectors point in the direction that increases their coordinate so the \(\hat{r}\) unit vector points in the direction of increasing \(r\text{,}\) and the \(\hat{\theta}\) unit vector points in the direction of increasing \(\theta\text{.}\) The direction of unit vectors in polar coordinates depends on position \(\left( r, \theta \right)\text{.}\) The fact that unit vectors for polar coordinates depend on position is usually undesirable. Let’s express them in terms of cartesian unit vectors which don’t depend on position to deal with this.
    🔗
    By projecting the \(\hat{r}\) and \(\hat{\theta}\) unit vectors onto the \(\hat{x}\) and \(\hat{y}\) unit vectors we get the following equations:
    \begin{equation*} \hat{r} = \cos (\theta) \hat{x} + \sin (\theta) \hat{y} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \hat{\theta} = - \sin (\theta) \hat{x} + \cos (\theta) \hat{y}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 18.2.2 Circular Motion

    Now we have the tools we need to relate linear and angular kinematics in circular motion.
    🔗

    Theorem 18.2.4.

    The velocity of an object undergoing circular motion is
    \begin{equation*} \vec{v} = r \omega \hat{\theta} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    For circular motion the position of the revolving object at any time \(t\) is
    \begin{equation*} \vec{r} = r \cos (\theta) \hat{x} + r \sin (\theta) \hat{x}. \end{equation*}
    Then
    \begin{equation*} \vec{v} = \frac{d \vec{r}}{dt} = -r \left( \frac{d\theta (t)}{dt} \right) \sin (\theta (t)) \hat{x} + r \left( \frac{d\theta (t)}{dt} \right) \cos (\theta (t)) \hat{y}. \end{equation*}
    Now \(\omega (t) = \frac{d \theta (t)}{dt}\) so we have
    \begin{align*} \vec{v} \amp = -r \omega (t) \sin (\theta (t)) \hat{x} + r \omega (t) \cos (\theta (t)) \hat{x} \\ \\ \amp = r \omega \hat{\theta}. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The speed of an object undergoing circular motion is then
    \begin{equation} v = r \omega.\tag{18.2.1} \end{equation}
    🔗

    Theorem 18.2.5.

    The acceleration of an object undergoing circular motion is
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} = r \alpha \hat{\theta} - r \omega^{2} \hat{r} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    \begin{align*} \vec{v} \amp = r \omega \hat{\theta} \\ \\ \amp = -r \omega (t) \sin (\theta (t)) \hat{x} + r \omega (t) \cos (\theta (t)) \hat{x} \end{align*}
    so
    \begin{align*} \vec{a} = \amp \frac{d \vec{v}}{dt}\\ \\ = \amp -r \left( \frac{d \omega (t)}{dt} \right) \sin (\theta (t)) \hat{x} -r \left( \omega (t) \right)^{2} \cos (\theta (t)) \hat{x}\\ \amp + r \left( \frac{d \omega (t)}{dt} \right) \cos (\theta (t)) \hat{y} -r \left( \omega (t) \right)^{2} \sin (\theta (t)) \hat{y} \end{align*}
    Now \(\alpha (t) = \frac{d \omega (t)}{dt}\) so
    \begin{align*} \vec{a} = \amp -r \alpha (t) \sin (\theta (t)) \hat{x} -r \left( \omega (t) \right)^{2} \cos (\theta (t)) \hat{x}\\ \amp + r \alpha (t) \cos (\theta (t)) \hat{y} -r \left( \omega (t) \right)^{2} \sin (\theta (t)) \hat{y}\\ \\ = \amp -r \alpha (t) \sin (\theta (t)) \hat{x} + r \alpha (t) \cos (\theta (t)) \hat{y}\\ \amp -r \left( \omega (t) \right)^{2} \cos (\theta (t)) \hat{x} -r \left( \omega (t) \right)^{2} \sin (\theta (t)) \hat{y}\\ \\ = \amp r \alpha (t) \left( - \sin (\theta (t)) \hat{x} + \cos (\theta (t)) \hat{y} \right)\\ \amp -r \left( \omega (t) \right)^{2} \left( \cos (\theta (t)) \hat{x} + \sin (\theta (t)) \hat{y} \right)\\ \\ = \amp r \alpha \hat{\theta} - r \omega^{2} \hat{r} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    If we break the acceleration vector into its componenents in polar coordinates we get
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} = a_{r} \hat{r} + a_{t} \hat{\theta} = r \alpha \hat{\theta} - r \omega^{2} \hat{r}. \end{equation*}
    Since vectors are equal if and only if their components are equal we see that the radial component of the acceleration \(a_{r}\) is given by
    \begin{equation} a_{r} = - r \omega^{2}\tag{18.2.2} \end{equation}
    and the angular component \(a_{t}\) is given by
    \begin{equation} a_{t} = r \alpha.\tag{18.2.3} \end{equation}
    The angular component of the acceleration \(a_{t}\) has a subscript \(t\) because its also known as the tangential acceleration. It called tangential acceleration because the angular component of the acceleration is always tangent to the object’s circular path at its current position.
    🔗
    \(a_{t}\) is also the component of the acceleration that changes the object’s angular velocity \(\omega\) over time. The radial component of the acceleration \(a_{r}\) is also known as the centripedal acceleration (The word centripedal means center seeking).
    🔗
    The radial acceleration always points towards the center of the circle. Plugging equation (18.2.1) into equation (18.2.2) gives another useful formula for the radial acceleration.
    \begin{align*} a_{r} \amp = - r \omega^{2} \\ \\ \amp = - r (\frac{v}{r})^{2} \\ \end{align*}
    \begin{equation} a_{r} = - \frac{v^2}{r}\tag{18.2.4} \end{equation}
    Finally, the radial and tangential components of the acceleration are always orthogonal (at a right angle to each other) so we can use the pythagorean theorem to get
    \begin{equation*} \lvert \vec{a} \rvert = \sqrt{a_{t}^{2} + a_{r}^{2}.} \end{equation*}
