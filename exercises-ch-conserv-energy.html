    1. Deriving the Gravitational Potential Energy formula, \(mgh\).

    2. Derivation of Conservaton of Energy.

    The Law of Conservation of Energy is actually just an alternative way of writing the Work-Energy Theorem. However, we are going to break down the net work into conservative work and non-conservative work.
    1. Write down the most general formula we have for net work done when the net force is not constant. Write the symbolic formula in terms of the net force vector, \({\vec{F}}_{net}\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} W_{net} = \int_{\fillinmath{X}}^{\fillinmath{X}} \fillinmath{XXXXX} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \({\vec{F}}_{net}\) is made up of two kinds of forces: conservative and non-conservative. Substitute in \({\vec{F}}_{net} = {\vec{F}}_{cons} + {\vec{F}}_{noncons}\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} W_{net} = \int_{\fillinmath{X}}^{\fillinmath{X}}{\fillinmath{XXXXXXXXXX}} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Break the integral into two parts, one for integrating the \({\vec{F}}_{cons}\text{,}\) and one for integrating the \({\vec{F}}_{noncons}\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} W_{net} = \int_{\fillinmath{X}}^{\fillinmath{X}}{\fillinmath{XXXXX}} + \int_{\fillinmath{X}}^{\fillinmath{X}}{\fillinmath{XXXXX}} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. From the definition of potential energy \(U\text{,}\) we know that the change in potential energy \(\Delta U\) is related to the \({\vec{F}}_{cons}\) by the following relationship:
      \begin{equation*} \mathrm{\Delta}U = - W_{cons} = - \int_{\fillinmath{X}}^{\fillinmath{X}}{\fillinmath{XXXXX}} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    5. From the Work-KE Theorem, we know that the net work is related to the change in kinetic energy by the following relationship:
      \begin{equation*} W_{net} = \fillinmath{XXXXX} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    6. Now, use your answers from steps 4 and 5 to substitute back into step 3. You can replace one of the integrals using your answer from step 4, and you can replace the \(W_{net}\) using your answer from step 5:
      \begin{equation*} \fillinmath{XXX} = \fillinmath{XXX} + \int_{\fillinmath{X}}^{\fillinmath{X}}{\fillinmath{XXXXX}} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    7. We can call the one remaining integral the work done by the non-conservative forces. (These include forces that move energy in/out of the isolated system, which could be called external work input, \(W_{ext}\text{.}\)) So,
      \begin{equation*} \mathrm{\Delta}KE = - \mathrm{\Delta}U + W_{ext} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    8. Now we can do some rearranging on the formula to clean it up. Note that \(\mathrm{\Delta}KE = {KE}_{f} - {KE}_{i}\) and \(\mathrm{\Delta}U = U_{f} - U_{i}\text{.}\) Substitute these in:
      \begin{equation*} \fillinmath{XX} - \fillinmath{XX} = - U_{f} + \fillinmath{XX} + W_{ext} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    9. Put all the energies that are evaluated using the final positions/velocities on the left-hand side; put all the energies that are evaluated using the initial positions/velocities on the right-hand side:
      \begin{equation*} {\fillinmath{XXX}}_{f} + {\fillinmath{XXX}}_{f} = {\fillinmath{XXX}}_{i} + {\fillinmath{XXX}}_{i} + W_{ext} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    10. \({KE}_{f} + U_{f}\) is defined as the final total mechanical energy: \(E_{f} = {KE}_{f} + U_{f}\text{.}\) \({KE}_{i} + U_{i}\) is defined as the initial total mechanical energy: \(E_{i} = {KE}_{i} + U_{i}\text{.}\) Subbing this in to shorten the equation, we have the Law of Conservation of Energy:
      \begin{equation*} E_{f} = E_{i} + W_{ext} \end{equation*}
      Note that often the \(W_{ext}\) is done by friction, and friction often does negative work. When \(W_{ext}\) is a negative number, \(E_{f}\) is less than \(E_{i}\) (in other words, energy was lost/dissipated as frictional heat). But if there is no nonconservative \(W_{ext}\text{,}\) then the final total energy is the same as the initial total energy. Note also that \(U\) is the total instantaneous potential energy from all sources of conservative work. It may be made up of many individual sources of potential energy. So, if gravity, electricity, and springs are all present in a problem, you will need to calculate the total instantaneous potential energy by breaking out
      \begin{equation*} U_{f}=U_{grav, f}+U_{elec, f}+U_{spring, f} \ \text{and} \ U_{i}=U_{grav, i}+U_{elec, i}+U_{spring, i}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    10. Power: the rate at which work is done by a force.

    Work transfers energy between things. The rate at which work is done, or the rate at which energy is transferred, is called the power.

Which method, A or B, accomplishes the \(2000\) joules of work at a faster rate?
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Since power is the rate at which work is done, which method had higher power? \(\fillinmath{X}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Knowing which method gave you higher power, which of the following equations must be the correct one?
      \begin{equation*} Average\ power = \frac{Work\ done}{Time\ elapsed} = \frac{\mathrm{\Delta}W}{\mathrm{\Delta}t} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} Average\ power = (Work\ done)(Time\ elapsed) = \mathrm{\Delta}W\mathrm{\Delta}t \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Assuming work done climbing the stairs was \(\mathrm{\Delta}W \approx 2000\) joules, what was the average power for the two cases? Check to make sure your answers agree with what you stated in question 2.
      🔗
      Average power for \(\mathrm{\Delta}t \approx 120\) seconds: \(\fillinmath{XXXXX}\)
      🔗
      Average power for \(\mathrm{\Delta}t \approx 10\) seconds: \(\fillinmath{XXXXX}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. The SI units for power are units of energy divided by units of time:
      \begin{equation*} Units\ of\ power:\ \frac{Units\ of\ energy}{Units\ of\ time} = \ \frac{\fillinmath{X}\ }{\fillinmath{X}\ },\ which\ is\ defined\ as\ a\ Watt\ (W). \end{equation*}
      Also note that if you know the power, but want the energy transferred, then \(\mathrm{\Delta}W = P\mathrm{\Delta}t\text{,}\) so you can invent a unit of energy by multiplying any unit of power times any unit of time. This is why your electricity company bills you for the amount of energy you used in units of kilowatts times \(\fillinmath{XXX}\) (abbreviated kWh).
      🔗
      🔗
    6. To get the instantaneous power, we take the limit of the average power as the time elapsed shrinks to zero:
      \begin{equation*} Instantaneous\ power\ P = \lim_{\mathrm{\Delta}t \rightarrow 0}\frac{Work\ done}{Time\ elapsed} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} P = \lim_{\mathrm{\Delta}t \rightarrow 0}\frac{\mathrm{\Delta}W}{\ \fillinmath{X}\ } \end{equation*}
      which turns into a derivative:
      \begin{equation*} P = \frac{dW}{d\ \fillinmath{X}\ } \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    7. We can actually write this in terms of the velocity, if the work is being done by a constant force \(\vec{F}\) and a straight-line displacement \(\vec{s}\text{.}\) We’ll need to sub in the formula for work if the force is constant, which was:
      \begin{equation*} W = \ \fillinmath{X}\ \cdot \vec{s} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    8. Subbing that in, we’ve got:
      \begin{equation*} P = \frac{d\ }{dt\ }\left( \fillinmath{X}\ \cdot \vec{s} \right) \end{equation*}
      If the force is constant, it factors outside the derivative:
      \begin{equation*} P = \fillinmath{X}\ \cdot \frac{d \left( \vec{s} \right)}{dt\ } \end{equation*}
      But we know that the derivative of the displacement \(\frac{d\ \vec{s}}{dt\ }\) is the \(\fillinmath{X}\text{,}\) so this formula can be written:
      \begin{equation*} P = \vec{F} \cdot \fillinmath{X}\ \end{equation*}
