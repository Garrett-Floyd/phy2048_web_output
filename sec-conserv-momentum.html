    1. Derivation of Conservation of Momentum in an isolated system.

    During a collision between two objects, it is usually too difficult to numerically calculate the non-conservative forces and the work they do. However, by examining the momentum vectors of the objects, we can still calculate something about the final velocities. Here, we will derive how the final velocities are related to the initial velocities.
    🔗
    1. 1. During a collision between body 1 and body 2, we know Newton’s Third Law says that \({\vec{F}}_{on2}\) (force of body 1 pushing on body 2) has to be the same magnitude as the back reaction \({\vec{F}}_{on1}\) (force of body 2 pushing on body \(\ \fillinmath{X}\)). The only difference between the forces is that the back reaction pushes body 1 in the opposite direction. To make the back reaction force the opposite direction, we put a \(\ \fillinmath{X}\) sign in front of it. Then, Newton’s Third Law says those forces are equal:
      \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{on2} = \ \ \fillinmath{X} \ {\vec{F}}_{on1} \end{equation*}
      To see what happens to the two target bodies during the collision, we need two FBD’s. The force on body 2 goes on the FBD for body 2. The force on body 1 goes separately on the FBD for body 1.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. 2. Let’s look at the \(F_{net}\) in the FBD’s. In our derivations of impulse, we found there was an alternative form of Newton’s Second Law, which related net force on a body to momentum of that body:
      \begin{equation*} \frac{d\vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }}{dt} = {\vec{F}}_{net} \end{equation*}
      Since the system is isolated, the only force contributing to the net force on body 1 during the collision is exerted by body \(\ \fillinmath{X}\text{.}\) Similarly, the only force contributing to the net force on body 2 during the collision is exerted by body \(\ \fillinmath{X}\text{.}\) So, we can easily sub in the symbolic \(F_{net}\) for the FBD of each body:
      🔗
      Body 1:
      \begin{equation*} \frac{d\vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }}{dt} = {\vec{F}}_{net,body1} = {\vec{F}}_{on1} \end{equation*}
      Therefore, calling the momentum vector of body 1 = \(\vec{p_{1}}\text{,}\) we see that:
      \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{on1} = \frac{d\vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }}{dt} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Body 2:
      \begin{equation*} \frac{d\vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }}{dt} = {\vec{F}}_{net,body2} = \ \fillinmath{X} \end{equation*}
      Therefore, calling the momentum vector of body 2 = \(\vec{p_{2}}\text{,}\) we see that:
      \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{on2} = \frac{d\vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }}{dt} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. 3. Now that we know the values of \({\vec{F}}_{on1}\) and \({\vec{F}}_{on2}\text{,}\) let’s sub that back into the equation we had from Newton’s Third Law in 1):
      \begin{equation*} {\vec{F}}_{on2} = \ - {\vec{F}}_{on1} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \frac{d\vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }}{dt} = - \frac{d\vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }}{dt} \end{equation*}
      Taking all terms to the left side,
      \begin{equation*} \frac{d\vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }}{dt} + \frac{d\vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ }}{dt} = 0 \end{equation*}
      “Factoring” out the derivative operator,
      \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}\left( \vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ } + \vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ } \right) = 0 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. 4. Each body is a source of momentum in the system. So, when we add up the momentum vectors of all the bodies, we call that the total momentum of the whole system: \(\vec{\ p_{total}} = \vec{\ p_{1}} + \vec{\ p_{2}}\text{.}\) So, subbing that in, we see:
      \begin{equation*} \frac{d}{dt}\left( \vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ } \right) = 0 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    5. 5. The derivative means the slope. What kind of function (quadratic? linear? constant? exponential?) has a slope of zero everywhere? \(\fillinmath{X}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    6. 6. So, this equation is saying that \(\vec{\ p_{total}}\) never changes. In other words, \(\vec{\ p_{total}}\) is conserved. This is called “The Law of Conservation of Momentum”. Since it never changes, here is how the initial momentum \({\vec{\ p_{total}}}_{i}\) and the final momentum \({\vec{\ p_{total}}}_{f}\) are related:
      \begin{equation*} {\vec{\ p_{total}}}_{i} = \ \fillinmath{X} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    7. 7. Or, breaking out the total momentum into all the sources it came from,
      \begin{equation*} {\vec{\ p_{1}}}_{i} + {\vec{\ \ \fillinmath{X}\ }}_{i} = {\vec{\ \ \fillinmath{X}\ }}_{f} + {\vec{\ p_{2}}}_{f} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    8. 8. Remember that the momentum is gotten by multiplying the velocity vector of the object by the mass
      \begin{equation*} m_{1}\vec{{v_{1}}_{i}} + m_{2}\vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ } = m_{1}\vec{\ \fillinmath{X}\ } + m_{2}\vec{{v_{2}}_{f}} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    9. 9. Fully breaking the vectors out into x and y components for the case of a 2-D velocity vector, we can do the x components and the y components of the vectors:
      \begin{equation*} m_{1}{{v_{1}}_{i}}_{x} + m_{2}{{v_{2}}_{i}}_{x} = m_{1}{{v_{1}}_{f}}_{x} + m_{2}{{v_{2}}_{f}}_{x} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} m_{1}{{v_{1}}_{i}}_{y} + m_{2}{{v_{2}}_{i}}_{y} = m_{1}{{v_{1}}_{f}}_{y} + m_{2}{{v_{2}}_{f}}_{y} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗

Count of total possible velocity component givens= \(\fillinmath{X}\text{.}\) That’s a lot of givens, not even counting the mass givens!
