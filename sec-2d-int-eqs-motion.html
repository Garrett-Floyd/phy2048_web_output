    Section 6.4 Integral Equations of Motion

    Theorem 6.4.1.

    \begin{equation*} \vec{r}_{f} = \int \vec{v}(t) d t+\vec{r}_{0} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Recall that
    \begin{equation*} v_{x}(t)\hat{x} + v_{y}(t)\hat{y} = \vec{v} = \frac{dx}{dt}\hat{x} + \frac{dy}{dt}\hat{y}. \end{equation*}
    Now vectors are equal if an only if there components are equal. For example, if the \(y\)-components of two vectors are different then they can’t be equal as vectors. So it must be the case that the \(x\) component of velocity on the left hand side of the equation is equal to the \(x\) component of the velocity on the right hand side of the equantion. The same thing goes for the \(y\) component of velocity on each side of the equation. Then
    \begin{equation*} v_{x}(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} v_{y}(t) = \frac{dy}{dt}. \end{equation*}
    Integrating the equation for the \(x\)-component gives
    \begin{align*} \frac{dx}{dt} \amp = v_{x}(t) \\ \\ \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} \frac{dx}{dt} dt \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} v_{x}(t) dt \\ \\ \int_{x_{i}}^{x_{f}} dx \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} v_{x}(t) dt \\ \\ x_{f} - x_{i} \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} v_{x}(t) dt \\ \\ x_{f} \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} v_{x}(t) dt + x_{i}. \\ \end{align*}
    Completely similarly, integrating the equation for the \(y\)-component gives
    \begin{equation*} y_{f} = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} v_{y}(t) dt + y_{i}. \end{equation*}
    Then
    \begin{align*} \vec{r}_{f} \amp = x_{f}\hat{x} + y_{f}\hat{y} \\ \\ \vec{r}_{f} \amp = \left( \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} v_{x}(t) dt + x_{i} \right)\hat{x} + \left( \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} v_{y}(t) dt + y_{i} \right)\hat{y} \\ \\ \vec{r}_{f} \amp = \left( \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} v_{x}(t) dt \hat{x} + \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} v_{y}(t) dt \hat{y} \right) + (x_{i}\hat{x} + y_{i}\hat{y}). \end{align*}
    Because \(\hat{x}\) and \(\hat{y}\) don’t change over time we can bring them into the integrals (not all unit vectors have this property). Contining we get
    \begin{align*} \vec{r}_{f} \amp = \left( \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} v_{x}(t) \hat{x} dt + \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} v_{y}(t) \hat{y} dt \right) + (x_{i}\hat{x} + y_{i}\hat{y}) \\ \\ \vec{r}_{f} \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} \left[ v_{x}(t) \hat{x} + v_{y}(t) \hat{y} \right] dt + (x_{i}\hat{x} + y_{i}\hat{y}) \\ \\ \vec{r}_{f} \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} \vec{v}(t) dt + \vec{r_{i}}. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 6.4.2.

    \begin{equation*} \vec{v}_{f} = \int \vec{a}(t) d t+\vec{v}_{0} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Recall that
    \begin{equation*} a_{x}(t)\hat{x} + a_{y}(t)\hat{y} = \vec{a} = \frac{dv_{x}}{dt}\hat{x} + \frac{dv_{y}}{dt}\hat{y}. \end{equation*}
    Now vectors are equal if an only if there components are equal. For example, if the \(y\)-components of two vectors are different then they can’t be equal as vectors. So it must be the case that the \(x\) component of velocity on the left hand side of the equation is equal to the \(x\) component of the velocity on the right hand side of the equantion. The same thing goes for the \(y\) component of velocity on each side of the equation. Then
    \begin{equation*} a_{x}(t) = \frac{dv_{x}}{dt} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} a_{y}(t) = \frac{dv_{y}}{dt}. \end{equation*}
    Integrating the equation for the \(x\)-component gives
    \begin{align*} \frac{dv_{x}}{dt} \amp = a_{x}(t) \\ \\ \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} \frac{dv_{x}}{dt} dt \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} a_{x}(t) dt \\ \\ \int_{v_{x,i}}^{v_{x, f}} dv_{x} \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} a_{x}(t) dt \\ \\ v_{x, f} - v_{x, i} \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} a_{x}(t) dt \\ \\ v_{x, f} \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} a_{x}(t) dt + v_{x, i}. \\ \end{align*}
    Completely similarly, integrating the equation for the \(y\)-component gives
    \begin{equation*} v_{y, f} = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} a_{y}(t) dt + v_{y, i}. \end{equation*}
    Then
    \begin{align*} \vec{v}_{f} \amp = v_{x, f}\hat{x} + v_{y, f}\hat{y} \\ \\ \vec{v}_{f} \amp = \left( \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} a_{x}(t) dt + v_{x, i} \right)\hat{x} + \left( \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} a_{y}(t) dt + v_{y, i} \right)\hat{y} \\ \\ \vec{v}_{f} \amp = \left( \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} a_{x}(t) dt \hat{x} + \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} a_{y}(t) dt \hat{y} \right) + (v_{x, i}\hat{x} + v_{y, i}\hat{y}). \end{align*}
    Because \(\hat{x}\) and \(\hat{y}\) don’t change over time we can bring them into the integrals (not all unit vectors have this property). Contining we get
    \begin{align*} \vec{v}_{f} \amp = \left( \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} a_{x}(t) \hat{x} dt + \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} a_{y}(t) \hat{y} dt \right) + (v_{x, i}\hat{x} + v_{y, i}\hat{y}) \\ \\ \vec{v}_{f} \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} \left[ a_{x}(t) \hat{x} + a_{y}(t) \hat{y} \right] dt + (v_{x, i}\hat{x} + v_{y, i}\hat{y}) \\ \\ \vec{v}_{f} \amp = \int_{t_{i}}^{t_{f}} \vec{a}(t) dt + \vec{v_{i}}. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    How should we interpret these equations? As stated before two vectors are equal if and only if their components are equal. Therefore the \(x\)-component of the left hand side of the equation in question is equal to the \(x\)-component of the right hand side of the \(x\)-component in question. The same is true for the \(y\)-components of each side of the equation in question.
